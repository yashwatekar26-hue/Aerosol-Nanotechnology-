AJP Process · Wilkinson & Smith 2019

① Atomization → ② Droplet size selection → ③ Sheath gas focusing & deposition
L1 Scan
What is the paper about?
Title: A review of aerosol jet printing — a non‑traditional hybrid process for micro‑manufacturing

Abstract: AJP is a contactless direct‑write approach for fine features on many substrates. It enables freeform deposition with a 1‑5 mm stand‑off, wide material compatibility, and high resolution.

Key Figures: Fig.1 shows the AJP process (atomizer → carrier gas → focusing → deposition). Fig.6 shows the parameters affecting deposition.
L2 Understand
What did they do & find?
What they did: They reviewed the AJP literature, summarizing atomization techniques, focusing mechanisms, and applications.

What they found:
  • Two atomization routes: Ultrasonic (1‑10 cP) and Pneumatic (1‑1000 cP, requires Virtual Impactor)
  • Sheath gas enables 1‑5 mm stand‑off → conformal printing
  • Finest lines: ~10 µm (Cai et al.)
  • Materials: silver, ceramics, SU‑8, proteins, DNA
  • Key challenges: overspray, empirical ink formulation
L3 Deep Dive
How & why does it matter?
How they did it: Literature review of AJP research from 2001‑2018. Organised by: atomization (ultrasonic vs pneumatic), focusing (sheath gas), materials (ink formulation), and applications.

What it contributes: This paper establishes the baseline for AJP mechanics. It shows the limitations — overspray, empirical ink formulation, ~10 µm resolution — that later papers address. P2 solves ink mixing, P3 improves resolution.
Source: Wilkinson et al. (2019) — Int J Adv Manuf Technol

Mid‑Air Blending · Hwang & Choi 2024

Separate atomization → combined at printhead → perfect dispersion with zero agglomeration
L1 Scan
What is the paper about?
Title: Highly dispersed nanomaterials in polymer matrix via aerosol‑jet‑based multi‑material 3D printing

Abstract: A novel AM3D printing method that achieves highly dispersed nanomaterials in a polymer matrix by atomizing polymer and nanoparticles separately, combining them only at the printhead.

Key Figures: Fig.1 shows the graffiti‑inspired process. Fig.2 shows SEM images of perfect dispersion. Fig.4 shows multi‑layer dielectric performance.
L2 Understand
What did they do & find?
What they did: They fabricated PI/Ag and PVDF‑TrFE/Ag nanocomposites using AM3D printing with separate atomization of polymer and nanoparticles.

What they found:
  • Zero agglomeration — SEM shows perfect dispersion
  • Dielectric constant (ε′): pure PVDF‑TrFE = 14 → nanocomposite = 28 (2×)
  • Multi‑layer structures achieved 16× higher ε′
  • TENG output: 80V → ~90V
  • Numerical simulation identified 114° junction angle as optimal
L3 Deep Dive
How & why does it matter?
How they did it: Pneumatic atomizer (PA) for polymer + Ultrasonic atomizer (UA) for Ag nanoparticles → separate streams combined at 114° Y‑junction → deposited as nanocomposite. Characterized by SEM, XRD, dielectric spectroscopy, and TENG.

What it contributes: This paper solves the 60‑year dispersion problem. It proves that the problem isn't the materials — it's the mixing process. It provides the multi‑material blending strategy that we propose to combine with P3's electrostatic focusing.
Source: Hwang et al. (2024) — Nano Energy

L1 Scan
What is the paper about?
Title: Three‑dimensional nanoprinting via charged aerosol focusing

Abstract: A novel 3D nano‑printing concept using a floating dielectric mask and electrostatic field to focus charged aerosols. Two regimes exist: growth mode and writing mode.

Key Figures: Fig.1 shows the mask + electrostatic focusing. Fig.2 shows growth vs writing modes. Fig.3 shows helix structures. Fig.4 shows writing of letters "3D".
L2 Understand
What did they do & find?
What they did: They developed Ion‑Assisted Aerosol Lithography (IAAL) — using a floating SiNₓ mask with ions creating electrostatic funnels to guide charged nanoparticles into 3D structures.

What they found:
  • Two modes: Growth mode (slow stage → pillars, helices) and Writing mode (fast stage → brush‑like deposition)
  • Resolution: < 100 nm — 100× smaller than conventional AJP
  • Multi‑material switching: Pd and Cu in same structure
  • Fabricated helices (1 µm radius), zig‑zags, stairs, and letters "3D"
  • "Persistence length" (Lp ≈ 1 µm) — theoretical concept to predict growth shape
L3 Deep Dive
How & why does it matter?
How they did it: Spark discharge generates charged nanoparticles (Pd, Cu, ~50 nm) → ions accumulate on SiNₓ mask (4 µm holes) → electric field funnels particles through holes → 3D stage controls growth (rate ~50 µm/h). Characterized by FE‑SEM, EDS, KFM.

What it contributes: This paper proves that true 3D nano‑printing is possible using electrostatic fields instead of physical nozzles. It provides the electrostatic focusing method that we propose to combine with P2's multi‑material blending.
Source: Jung et al. (2019) — Science (preprint)

Section 03 · The missing link

Where do they connect?

Each paper solves a specific piece. The gap is where they don't overlap.

Current approaches remain fragmented:

  • Hwang et al. (2024) demonstrates multi‑material blending (AM3D) but is limited to micro‑scale 2.5D structures (> 1 µm).
  • Jung et al. (2019) achieves sub‑100 nm 3D nano‑structuring (IAAL) but strictly with single metals (Pd, Cu).
  • No single process has unified dynamic in‑flight multi‑material blending with electrostatically guided vertical nano‑construction.

Section 04 · The next step

One question. Built from the gaps.

This is not a question from the papers — it is one we formed after reading all three.

"Can the pneumatic/ultrasonic dual‑atomization mid‑air blending strategy of Hwang et al. (AM3D) be integrated with the ion‑induced electrostatic focusing lens of Jung et al. (IAAL) to enable the single‑pass fabrication of compositionally graded, sub‑100 nm resolution 3D nano‑pillars and helices?"

Why we ask it

  • ✓ P3 proves sub‑100 nm 3D but only with single metals.
  • ✓ P2 proves perfect multi‑material mixing but at micro‑scale.
  • ✓ Each solves part of the problem — no one has joined all three.

Where this could go

  • ✓ 3D Nano‑electronics — multi‑material interconnects at the molecular scale.
  • ✓ Heterogeneous sensors — dielectric + conductor in one 3D nano‑architecture.
  • ✓ Metamaterials — optical/mechanical properties impossible with single materials.

Our honest opinion:

If we had to pick the most impactful paper, it is P2 — Hwang & Choi 2024. P1 and P3 prove beautiful ideas, but P2 solves a foundational materials problem that has limited the entire field for decades. That said, P3 is the most exciting concept — electrostatically guided 3D nano‑printing could define the next generation of additive manufacturing. And P1 is the foundation the others build on.

Together they tell one story: P1 brings the mechanics, P3 brings the nano‑resolution, and P2 shows how materials can be blended perfectly.
