Paper 1 · Review

Micro-Manufacturing Systems

Wilkinson & Smith (2019) — Int J Adv Manuf Technol

📄 Read Abstract

"Aerosol Jet Printing (AJP) is an emerging contactless direct write approach… Freeform deposition, coupled with a relatively large stand-off distance, enables increased geometric complexity. Wide material compatibility, high resolution and independence of orientation provide novelty… This overview summarises the principles, reviews applications and discusses hurdles to industry adoption."

Core Mechanics: AJP Process

① Liquid atomization → ② Droplet size selection (Virtual Impactor) → ③ Sheath gas focusing & coaxial deposition

This work breaks down the core mechanics of particle aerosol generation and the coaxially directed deposition mechanism via high-velocity shroud gas and virtual impaction. It establishes a foundational reference for structural flexibility and non-contact surface jetting.

Two Atomization Routes

Ultrasonic (1–10 cP) for monodisperse droplets. Pneumatic (1–1000 cP) for high viscosity, requiring a virtual impactor.

Extreme Versatility

Silver, ceramics, SU‑8, proteins, DNA – all printable with the same hardware.

Key Bottleneck

Overspray (satellite droplets) and empirical ink formulation (no universal "Z‑number").

Reported finest lines: ~10 µm. Resistances ~2× bulk silver. Bending endurance < 10 000 cycles.

Paper 2 · Innovation

Aerosol-Jet Multi-Material 3D Printing

Hwang & Choi (2024) — Nano Energy

📄 Read Abstract

"Polymer-based nanocomposites emerged in the 1960s… however, achieving uniform nanomaterial dispersion has impeded progress. Here, we present a polymer-based nanocomposite with highly dispersed nanomaterials, achieved through aerosol-jet-based multi-material three-dimensional (AM3D) printing. Numerical simulations facilitate the avoidance of interfacial compatibility issues… the 3D structured nanocomposite exhibits a uniform dielectric constant and low dielectric loss."

This approach achieves homogeneous material blending mid-flight, bypassing conventional liquid-state pre-mixing. The research unlocks energy harvesting and fully printed dielectric composites with uniform electrical response.

Dispersion: Old Problem vs. New Solution

❌ Pre‑Mixing (Agglomeration)

Nanoparticles clump → weak spots, electrical leakage

✅ Mid‑Air Blending (AM3D)

Perfect dispersion, uniform properties

Left: Clustered agglomeration from pre‑mixing. Right: Isotropic dispersion via separate atomization.

The "Graffiti" Trick

Atomize polymer and nanoparticles separately; combine only at the printhead. No pre‑mixing → no agglomeration.

16× Dielectric Boost

Single‑layer ε′ rose from 14 to 28. Multi‑layer sandwiches achieved 16× higher ε′ with low loss.

TENG Output

Voc increased from ~80 V (pure) to ~90 V for optimized nanocomposite.

Paper 3 · Breakthrough

Three-Dimensional Nanoprinting

Jung & Choi (2019) — Science (preprint)

📄 Read Abstract

"We propose a novel 3D nano-printing concept… We apply a floating dielectric mask to focus charged aerosols through a convergent electrostatic field… we found two different regimes (3D growth mode and 3D writing mode) exist depending on the translation velocity of the stage."

This advances structural depth by using electrostatic polarization to direct charged particle streams into vertical micro-pillars and helix structures, enabling additive manufacturing at the molecular scale.

Electrostatic Focusing Architecture

x) Growing 3D Nanopillar Substrate
The ion cushion (glowing amber cloud) creates an electrostatic lens. Charged particles focus through the mask hole onto a growing 3D nanopillar.

Electrostatic "Funnel"

Ions accumulate on the mask, creating a local field that guides particles with < 100 nm precision.

Two Modes

Growth: slow stage → pillars, helices. Writing: fast stage → brush‑like letters "3D".

100× Smaller

Resolution < 100 nm vs. conventional AJP’s ~10 µm – a leap into true 3D nano‑architectures.

Research Gap

Where Do They Connect?

Current approaches remain fragmented:

  • Hwang et al. (2024) demonstrates multi‑material blending (AM3D) but is limited to micro‑scale 2.5D structures (> 1 µm).
  • Jung et al. (2019) achieves sub‑100 nm 3D nano‑structuring (IAAL) but strictly with single metals (Pd, Cu).
  • No single process has unified dynamic in‑flight multi‑material blending with electrostatically guided vertical nano‑construction.
